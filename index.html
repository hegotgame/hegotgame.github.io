Introduction

I work for a local food manufacturer. We have many food and beverage products. Despite working with the materials in a hands-on manner, in writing up this post, I realized I am only a small cog in the machine in producing the product shown above! My company batches some raw materials, bottles the batch up, and labels the bottles. We are not the first or last touchpoint in the supply chain, and I realized so much work and materials go into any product we produce. What environmental impacts were associated with the production of your meal or item? One specific environmental impact I can speak on is that the plant I work in is on the TRI (Toxic Release Inventory) list we reviewed in class. My plant uses numerous hazardous chemicals (mostly extremely strong acids and bases). The primary reasons we need to do this involves the rigorous cleaning and sanitizing standards we implement in production and the chemicals are needed to clean and sanitize our equipment depending on time, conditions, and circumstances. There are many impacts associated with such responsibility associated with hazardous chemicals. These impacts include handling, labeling, worker safety, disposal, release, and chemical mixing hazards. For example, I’ve only worked for this company for under a year, but I heard about a significant event that had direct environmental impact on the local community. You can read about it here. (a) Determine and describe, in as much detail as you can, where at least two ingredients of your meal OR components of your consumer item were produced. (You can do more if you are so inspired.) Two ingredients I want to focus on are water and ascorbic acid. Water is the one ingredient that I am personally reasonable for adding and, yes, it is the ingredient with the highest quantity! We have two “types” of water we add, depending on customer requirements. We either add well water from underground Germantown or deionized water from our water silo. I am not sure if the DI water is filled up by an outside vendor or produced on site, but either possibility is within our capabilities. The well water is supposedly a famous property of Wisconsin water as being tasty (or maybe I’m just buying into the hype train, but I can say from personal experience that the water is very good)! I don’t deal directly with any of the other ingredients, but I know that the ascorbic acid sometimes must be adjusted if the product formula needs to be tweaked to match customer requirements. Again, this is another hazardous chemical that needs to be handled, but at least it is a relatively weak acid. Sometimes we run out of water! DI water, that is. Well water is being pumped out in large quantities, we have several water meters to draw from, each meter is capable of drawing approximately 3600 gallons of water per hour! I did a “Climate Change Hackathon” earlier this week and I learned that the average American uses 82 gallons of water in a day. Well, I guess I’m not that average when I draw from the well! This water doesn’t all go into my stomach and it actually gets distributed all over the world. Some of our products are very popular in the Middle East, for example. So yes, I pump the water out of the well and it might go into a cheese or beverage that gets eaten in Dubai! We may be depleting the water supply, but I would need to do further analysis to sustain that claim as there are natural cycles that replace some of what we draw out. I also monitor the pH as we add water to our products and the changing pH components eventually get released to the environment, which could possibly alter ecosystems if not done in a safe way.
